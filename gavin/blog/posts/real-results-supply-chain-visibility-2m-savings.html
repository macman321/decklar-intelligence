Real Results: How Supply Chain Visibility Saved a Customer $2M

Gavin • May 14, 2026 • 10 min read
← Back to Home

Note: This is a real case study from one of our enterprise customers. While I'm keeping their identity confidential per their request, every number, every challenge, and every outcome described here is absolutely real. This is what happens when supply chain visibility moves from "nice to have" to "mission critical."

I still remember the first meeting with their VP of Supply Chain. Let's call him Michael. He walked into our conference room looking like someone who hadn't slept well in months—and given what he was dealing with, I can't blame him.

His company, a global manufacturer of high-value electronic components, was hemorrhaging money. Not from declining sales or rising material costs, but from something far more insidious: they simply didn't know where their products were. Shipments were disappearing, arriving damaged, or showing up weeks late. Insurance claims were piling up. Customers were threatening to leave. And the worst part? They had no idea what was actually happening out there in the supply chain abyss.

The Problem: Flying Blind in a $500M Operation

Michael's company ships approximately $500 million worth of electronic components annually. These aren't just any electronics—they're specialized semiconductors, precision sensors, and critical components that power everything from medical devices to aerospace systems. The average shipment value? Around $150,000. Some individual containers hold over $2 million in cargo.

Before Decklar, their visibility was limited to what carriers told them—and carriers, being human (and having their own interests to protect), weren't always forthcoming. A shipment might show "in transit" for three weeks with no actual location data. Temperature-sensitive cargo could arrive with no way to prove whether it had been compromised. Shipments would simply vanish into the logistics void, only to resurface weeks later—or never.

The annual losses were staggering:

  • $890,000 in lost or stolen cargo
  • $430,000 in temperature excursions that ruined sensitive electronics
  • $380,000 in expedited shipping costs to cover for delayed deliveries
  • $250,000 in chargebacks and penalties from major customers
  • $120,000 in insurance deductibles and claims processing

That's nearly $2.1 million in preventable losses every single year. And that's not counting the reputational damage, the strained customer relationships, or the sleepless nights.

The Solution: Total Visibility from Door to Door

We started with a pilot program—because nobody wants to bet the farm on a technology they haven't tested. Michael selected 50 high-value shipments for our proof-of-concept. Each got a Bee Label, configured with custom alert thresholds for their specific cargo types.

Within the first week, we caught our first problem. A $200,000 shipment headed to a major automotive customer was sitting in a warehouse in Rotterdam for three days—without any movement or updates from the carrier. The Bee Label's geofence alert triggered, notifying Michael's team immediately. They contacted the carrier, discovered a paperwork error, and got the shipment moving again. Estimated savings on that single catch: $15,000 in expedited shipping costs and potentially a major customer relationship.

By week three, we had identified systemic issues that were invisible before:

  • One of their "reliable" carriers had a pattern of leaving cargo in non-climate-controlled facilities during layovers, causing temperature excursions.
  • A specific shipping route through Eastern Europe had a theft problem that nobody had connected to the carrier—they were using subcontractors who weren't properly vetting drivers.
  • Their own warehouse was maintaining temperatures 5°C higher than specified during afternoon hours—a HVAC issue nobody had noticed.

The Full Implementation

Convinced by the pilot results, Michael's company rolled out Decklar visibility across their entire operation. Over six months, we deployed Bee Labels on every shipment over $25,000 in value—about 2,400 devices in rotation.

The integration went deeper than just tracking. We connected the Decklar API to their existing ERP system, creating a unified dashboard that their logistics team, customer service representatives, and even executives could access. Now, when a customer called asking about their order, the service rep could see the exact location, current temperature, and estimated arrival time in real time.

But the real transformation was in their operational approach:

Proactive vs. Reactive

Before, Michael's team spent their days putting out fires—frantic calls to carriers, emergency expedited shipments, angry customer conversations. After implementation, they became proactive. Alerts gave them hours or days of advance warning, letting them intervene before problems became crises.

Data-Driven Negotiations

Armed with irrefutable data about carrier performance, Michael's team renegotiated contracts with three major logistics providers. When a carrier claimed their 98% on-time delivery rate, Michael showed them the actual data: 87%. The resulting contract renegotiations saved $340,000 annually.

Insurance Wins

When a $450,000 shipment was damaged during ocean transit, the Bee Label data proved the damage occurred during a specific port transfer where the container was dropped. The carrier tried to deny responsibility, but the shock sensor data and timeline made liability clear. Full claim paid in 3 weeks instead of the usual 6-month battle.

Customer Confidence

Major customers started getting proactive notifications: "Your shipment is 2 hours from your facility," "Temperature maintained within specification throughout transit," "Arrived at destination, awaiting customs clearance." Customer satisfaction scores jumped 23%, and they started winning RFPs they used to lose.

The Results: One Year Later

Twelve months after full implementation, I sat down with Michael again. This time, he looked rested. Actually, he looked triumphant.

The numbers told an incredible story:

  • Lost/stolen cargo reduced by 94% — from $890K to $53K. Most remaining losses were from shipments that didn't have Bee Labels (they're now deploying on everything over $5K).
  • Temperature excursions eliminated — from $430K to $12K (a few false positives from configuration errors).
  • Expedited shipping costs cut by 78% — from $380K to $84K. Only used when absolutely necessary.
  • Customer penalties and chargebacks: zero — from $250K to $0. Not a single late delivery penalty in 12 months.
  • Insurance claims down 82% — from $120K to $22K. Most claims now handled in under 30 days with clear data.

Total annual savings: $2,079,000

That doesn't include the soft benefits: improved customer relationships, competitive advantage in sales, reduced stress for the logistics team, and the ability to sleep soundly at night knowing their cargo is actually being monitored.

The ROI Conversation

I know what you're thinking: "Sure, but what did this cost?" Fair question.

Their annual investment in Decklar—hardware, platform licenses, API integration, and support—was approximately $280,000. That's a 7.4x return in the first year alone. And since Bee Labels are reusable and the platform scales efficiently, that ROI only improves over time.

Michael told me something that stuck with me: "The crazy thing is, we were already paying for visibility. We just weren't getting it. We were paying for insurance that barely covered our losses, expedited shipping to fix problems we couldn't prevent, and losing customers we couldn't retain. Decklar didn't add cost—it replaced hidden costs with a predictable investment that actually delivers."

Lessons for Everyone

Every supply chain is different, but some principles are universal:

Visibility isn't a luxury—it's risk management. If you don't know where your cargo is, you can't protect it. It's that simple.

Data beats hope every time. Trusting that carriers will take care of your cargo is betting on hope. Having sensors that prove it is investing in certainty.

Proactive beats reactive. Catching problems early saves money. Solving crises late costs money. The math is undeniable.

Your customers notice. In a world where everyone else is guessing, being the company that actually knows what's happening is a massive competitive advantage.
